Substance Use Disorders

  • Chronic conditions
  • Initial treatments are effective
  • 1 in 10 individuals receive treatment
  • Episodic approach to care
  • Treatment disparities exist

Substance use disorders are chronic conditions with high relapse rates, substantial co-morbidity with mental and physical health issues, and increased mortality risk.

We have effective Medication and psychosocial interventions. But, nearly 90% of people with a substance use disorder do not receive any form of treatment.

For those who do receive care, treatment is often brief and episodic. Many individuals enter care only when symptoms are severe and risk is high. At the same time, services are time-limited, resources are constrained, and providers frequently operate at or beyond capacity. As a result, patients often leave treatment without adequate supports in place, contributing to a “revolving door” pattern in which individuals cycle repeatedly through acute episodes of care.

These limitations are magnified for people from certain demographic subgroups who may face unique barriers to treatment, including financial constraints, limited service availability, and mobility or transportation challenges. And marginalized groups may additionally experience minority stress, stigma, discrimination, and isolation leading to higher rates of substance use disorders and poorer treatment outcomes.

Our treatment for substance use disorders stands in stark contrast to how we treat other chronic conditions.

When someone has diabetes, we would never expect a brief episode of care to be sufficient; instead, we rely on ongoing monitoring, adherence to treatment, regular clinical follow-up, and healthy lifestyle choices to manage risk over time.

Substance use disorders are no different—except in how we treat them.

Continuing Care

  • Continuing care is gold-standard for chronic conditions
  • Current SUD treatment infrastructure lacks capacity for long-term clinician-delivered care
  • Individuals are left to navigate recovery alone

Continuing care is the gold standard for chronic health conditions. Similarly, substance use treatment is most effective when provided over longer durations and with active efforts to keep patients engaged in their recovery.

But our current system simply does not have the capacity to deliver long‑term, clinician‑led care at scale.

Without ongoing support, people are left to navigate recovery on their own, asking: How risky is today? What do I need right now? Which recovery tool should I use and when?

Relapse Prevention

  • Recovery is a non-linear process
  • Lapses serve as early warning signs of relapse
  • Risk factors differ across individuals and within individuals over time
  • Self-monitoring is difficult

The challenge is recovery is not linear.

Lapses, or instances of goal‑inconsistent substance use, are common and and can occur at any point during recovery. On their own, they do not constitute relapse. But they must precede relapse, making them meaningful early warning signs.

The relapse prevention literature has identified a wide range of precipitants of lapse. But these risk factors fluctuate from day-to-day and differ across individuals and within individuals over time.

They interact with one another and with interpersonal and environmental situations to influence lapse risk.

So given this complex nature of lapse risk, self-monitoring is difficult.

Recovery Monitoring and Support System

  • Personalized adaptive recommendations
  • Prompt individuals to engage with support at times of high risk
  • Scalable to address unmet need

An algorithm-guided recovery monitoring and support system could help individuals monitor their current and future risk of lapse after initial treatment and adjust their lifestyle, behaviors and support accordingly.

Such a system could offer personalized, adaptive recommendations aligned with evidenced-based care and prompt individuals to engage with support at times of high risk.

For example, individuals could receive daily messages about changes in their lapse risk, along with recommendations based on their key risk factors, like an urge-surfing recommendation for someone experiencing strong cravings.

Moreover, it would provide a scalable option for long-term monitoring, addressing the substantial unmet need for continuing care in substance use disorder treatment.

Recovery Monitoring and Support System

  1. Collect densely sampled risk-relevant data
  1. Access an accurate and interpretable model
  1. Perform fairly

For such a system to exist, we need at least three things. One, the system must be able to collect a rich and densely sampled source (or sources) of risk-relevant data.

Two, the system must have access to a model that can predict substance use with high performance and have interpretable model inputs for support recommendations to be mapped onto.

Three, the model must perform fairly. Algorithms that perform favorably for only a single majority group could widen existing disparities in access to resources and important clinical outcomes

Sensing Data and Machine Learning

  • 91% of Americans own a smartphone
  • At least 85% ownership across across gender, race/ethnicity, socioeconomic status, and geographic location subgroups
  • Frequent longitudinal measurement of proximal risk
  • An array of techniques from machine learning to predict lapse risk and highlight prospective risk factors

Advances in both smartphone sensing and machine learning now make this possible.

The proliferation of smartphone usage in recent years makes smartphone sensing a potentially scalable and equitable method for improving lapse risk monitoring. As of 2025, roughly 91% of Americans own a smartphone and smartphone ownership is consistently high across important demographic subgroups.

Smartphone sensing approaches can provide the frequent, longitudinal measurement of proximal risk factors that is necessary for prediction of future lapses with high temporal precision.

Machine learning models can handle high dimensional sensing feature sets and can accommodate non-linear and interactive relationships between features and lapse probability. And methods from interpretable machine learning can be used to understand which risk features contribute most strongly to a lapse prediction for a specific individual at a specific moment in time.

Two sensing methods I am particularly excited about are EMA and geolocation sensing.

Ecological Momentary Assessment

  • Direct and frequent insight into subjective feelings and experiences
  • Constructs easily map onto well-studied risk factors for lapse
  • Appears to be well-tolerated

Ecological momentary assessment, or EMA, consists of daily or more frequent brief surveys optimized for someone to complete on their smartphone. This signal is unique in that it provides privileged access into subjective momentary experiences and allows for measuring several face-valid constructs of the relapse prevention model

e.g., we can ask people to directly report their craving, their level of self-efficacy, and their affect.

EMA appears to be well tolerated with adherence rates in substance using populations being comparable to other clinical samples.

Geolocation

  • Passively sensed
  • May provide insight into information difficult to measure with self-report
  • Locations can be contextualized

Geolocation sensing is a passive sensing method that could complement EMA well. It may provide a distinct set of intervenable features from information that is difficult to measure with self-report or that would add additional burden by increasing the number of questions on daily surveys.

e.g., low location variance or increased time spent at home could signal reduced mobility, social withdrawal, or disengagement

geolocation features may become even more powerful when combined with contextual information from public sources (e.g., census data, alcohol outlet density data) or from self-report (e.g., self-evaluated riskiness of specific locations).

Today I am going to present a series of studies aimed at improving continuing care for SUDS by demonstrating the feasibility of building an automated recovery monitoring and support system. Across three studies, I will address the key features needed for such a system, including assessing potential sensing methods for their ability to predict lapse accurately and timely and evaluating the interpretability and fairness of model outputs.

Specific Aims

  1. Evaluate and interpret models that predict immediate risk of a lapse back to alcohol use using 4x daily EMA

  2. Evaluate and interpret models that predict future risk of a lapse back to alcohol use using 4x daily EMA

  3. Evaluate and interpret models that predict immediate risk of a lapse back to opioid use using 1x daily EMA and geolocation sensing

To achieve this goal, we used two clinical samples of participants in early recovery from alcohol use disorder (Studies 1–2) and opioid use disorder (Study 3) with reported goals of abstinence.

In Study 1, we evaluated and interpreted three separate models that provided probabilities of an immediate lapse back to alcohol use with increasing temporal precision: lapses in the next week, next day, and next hour, using features engineered from a 4X daily EMA. These immediate risk models can provide personalized support recommendations to address risks for possible lapses in that moment.

In Study 2, we evaluated and interpreted models that use the same 4× daily EMA features to predict alcohol lapses occurring within a 24-hour window up to two weeks into the future. These models with lagged prediction windows provide insight into lapse risk with the added benefit of advanced warning, giving individuals extra time to make behavioral, social, or lifestyle changes.

In Study 3, we extended lapse prediction to opioid use using a national sample of participants with opioid use disorder. This allowed us to evaluate whether lapse prediction algorithms generalize beyond alcohol to substances where use is illegal and lapses may be underreported. We also supplemented EMA with geolocation to assess an alternative less budensome sensing method.

Across all three studies we evaluated the interpretability and fairness of model outputs.

Design

  • Outcome = lapse or no lapse
  • XGBoost algorithm
  • Grouped nested k-fold cross-validation
  • True prediction into the future

There are a couple of additional methods details that are important to know and they apply to all three studies.

The start time and day of substance use reported on EMA was used to generate our outcome labels of lapse or no lapse.

We considered several candidate statistical algorithms, but for all studies the best performing algorithm was XGBoost.

We used grouped nested cross validation – this method produces INDEPENDENT, UNBIASED estimates of how well the model performs in NEW DATA That means the data used for model evaluation was not used to train the models. Our data was also grouped by participant, so all of a participant’s data was either in training or test.

Lastly, all of our features used in the models were collected before the lapse onset. So when I say predict I mean we are predicting on new data from new individuals but we are also predicting into the future.

  • 95% compliance for completing at least one survey daily

Participants on average completed 3 of the 4 EMAs each day and completed at least one EMA on 95.0% of days.

Performance

So we’ve established that we can predict lapse at different levels of precision.

But lets briefly zoom back out to our broader goal—building a recovery monitoring and support system that communicates risk to individuals.

A weekly prediction window is likely too coarse to be actionable, while an hourly model would require delivering risk updates every hour, which is not practical for implementation.

In contrast, the next day (or next 24-hour) prediction window strikes a balance between these two extremes. It allows us to provide timely, meaningful risk information without overwhelming the individual.

Moving forward, I am going to focus exclusively on models with 24-hour prediction windows.

Model Fairness (Next-day)

We explicitly evaluated how fair our next day models performed for three important subgroups with known disparities in access to substance use treatment and substance use outcomes – race and ethnicity (Hispanic and/or not White vs. non-Hispanic White), income (below poverty vs. above poverty), and sex at birth (female vs. male).

Model Fairness (Next-day)

Our models performed worse for participants who were not white and had an income below the federal poverty line.

The largest contributing factor is likely the lack of diversity in our training data.

Even with our coarse dichotomous grouping of race we only had 20 participants in the not White group (compared to 131 in the White group). We also saw a similar pattern in our income groups (with 49 people in the below poverty group and 102 people in the above poverty group).

Model Fairness (Next-day)

We did however have equal representation of men and women, and we still saw differences in performance. This was a bit surprising. I am not sure exactly why this difference exists but one hypothesis is that it is related to the features themself. We chose our EMA items using domain expertise from decades of research on the factors that predict relapse. However, up until 1993 women were mostly excluded from substance use treatment research. As a result, it is possible that these constructs more accurately predict relapse risk for men than women. And of course it is possible that this also applies to other groups under-represented in the literature.

Feature Importance (Next-day)

We can use metrics of feature importance to understand which features are influencing our models predictions on average.

Since our best performing model was XGBoost we calculated Shapley values. Shapley values indicate how much a feature score for a specific observation adjusts the prediction from the mean score across all observations.

Feature Importance (Next-day)

We aggregated the shapley values by calculating the mean absolute shapley value across all observations to see how much each feature, on average, contributed to the next day model’s predictions.

Past use was the most important feature. This is not surprising given that our outcome was lapse, and past behavior is often the best predictor of future behavior.

Future efficacy emerged as second most important, indicating that participants had reasonably accurate insight into their near-term abstinence success. Craving was also an important predictor that may be an important target for early recovery support.

As a reminder this approach summarizes overall feature importance by averaging the magnitude of each feature’s contribution. A down-side is that it can be skewed toward features that exhibit infrequent but very large Shapley values. So for example less than 8% of observations contained lapses and almost half of our participants didn’t lapse at all on study. This means that past use may not actually represent what feature was most important on most days for most individuals.

So perhaps what we really want to know is how often these features came up as the most important feature on a given day for a given individual.

Feature Importance (Next-day)

The plot on the right summarises the proportion of observations in which each feature had the highest Shapley value. So now considering the magnitude of the influence and the prevalance.

This plot shows that although past use was the most influential feature overall, it was only the most influential feature on a subset of days.

In fact, past and future self-reported stressful events came up as the most important feature on the most days despite this having a small influence on predictions globally.

This shows that that while some features may have low average importance (across all individuals and observations), they can be highly important on certain days for certain individuals and are worthwhile to keep in the model for delivering feedback personalized to the person and the moment.

Conclusions

  • We can predict probabilities of lapse at varying levels of temporal precision with excellent performance
  • We can characterize the relative importance of features contributing to these risk probabilities for specific individuals at specific moments in time
  • A lack of diversity in the training data makes these models not yet ready for implementation
  • Well-suited for personalized treatment recommendations that can be done immediately

Here are the average contributions of each feature to the model’s predictions for our immediate model from study 1 in red and our two week lagged model in grey. So this is that traditional characterization of overall feature importance by mean absolute shapley value.

Now for study three we are going to switch datasets. In this study we developed a machine learning model that predicts next-day opioid lapse risk using daily self-report and geolocation sensing data.

A couple differences with this dataset aside from substance of use:

First, we recruited a national sample of participants across the United States in attempt to increase diversity of our participants.

Second, participants provided sensing data and opioid lapse reports for up to one year, a duration that exceeds previously published substance use prediction studies and allowed us to capture longer-term recovery trajectories.

Participants (N = 296)

Our sample consisted of 296 participants in the US in early recovery from opioid use disorder. To be eligible, participants must have been enrolled in a medication for OUD program or recently completed a residential treatment program. The majority of our participants had severe opioid use disorder.

Participants (N = 296)

Our sample was predominantly White, but had some more representation of Black and Hispanic participants compared to our previous sample. We had slightly more men than women.

Participants (N = 296)

Most participants were between 26-45 years and had an annual household income below $25,000

  • Participants completed 1 daily survey

    • Depressed, angry, anxious, relaxed, and happy
    • Sleep quality
    • MOUD adherence
    • Motivation and confidence
    • Opioid and other drug and alcohol use

Participants completed 1X daily surveys for up to 12 months

Performance

Our full model that included both sets of sensing features performed very well with an overall auROC of 0.94.

To understand the incremental predictive value of EMA and GPS sensing features, we also evaluated performance of models that ablated each set of features individually and together.

Performance

Model performance was comparable when GPS features were removed from the model. And performance dramatically fell when ema features were removed on their own or in combination with GPS features. This suggests that GPS data may not be adding any signal above and beyond what EMA is capturing. That being said GPS did capture some signal, with the ablated EMA model performing still above chance at .63.

Model Fairness

We evaluated our full model across five dichotomous subgroups - gender, income, race and ethnicity, sexual orientation, and geographic location.

There was evidence that model performance differed for all group comparisons. Notably, our model performed better for individuals with an annual income of less than $25,000 compared to individuals with an annual income greater than $25,000, thus favoring the disadvantaged group.

While differences in performance estimates exist, this model is likely still clinically meaningful across subgroups, as all subgroups yielded median auROCs between 0.91 - 0.95

And it certainly is much better than our models in studies 1-2

Conclusions

  • EMA is a feasible even when measured for up to a year
  • GPS does not offer any unique incremental predictive value beyond EMA
  • It may still be worthwhile to include GPS features

In study 3 we found that

EMA remains to be a feasible and predictive signal even when measured for up to a year.

If the goal is prediction, GPS features are not necessary as they do not offer any unique incremental predictive value beyond EMA.

However, if the goal is to have a more nuanced understanding of lapse risk across a more diverse array of risk features than GPS is helpful. Adding GPS features did not hurt model performance and several of them emerged in the top subset of features.

So although GPS features may not provide unique signal for prediction, it may be worthwhile to still include them in the model to expand opportunities for intervention and personalized recovery support.

In terms of where to go next. One consideration might be to extend the number of risk features we are measuring to increase our ability to personalize recommendations.

Future Directions

  • Expand risk features

EMA was shown to be a robust sensing method for lapse prediction even when dropped to only 1 daily survey.

We could imagine adding additional questions to our EMA. This could potentially improve model performance for subgroups where performance disparities still existed, such as with race, orientation, and geographic location.

I did find it interesting that fairness improved for women in study 3 and it makes me wonder if this is related to some of the additional items included on our EMA or from GPS.

However, we can’t add too many additional items without making our survey much more burdensome for people to complete.

Future Directions

  • Expand risk features

  • Improve GPS sensing

One way to expand risk features without increasing burden is to supplement self-report data with more passive sensing methods. We started to do this with GPS data in study 3.

And while our results may appear somewhat discouraging for its predictive value it does much better than other passive methods I have explored like cellular communications. So I believe theres still a case to be made for using GPS data and we had some methodological limitations worth noting.

First Although gps data can, in principle, be collected continuously, in practice these data are difficult to collect reliably. In our study, we had much higher rates of missingness for gps features compared to ema features.

Second, our feature engineering pipeline relied on known locations where people provided self-reported context. Passive methods for contextualizing location could provide risk-relevant information across the full range of locations people visit.

Future Directions

  • Improve GPS sensing

For example, Chris Janssen and others in our lab have begun to explore how GPS coordinates could be enriched using publicly available datasets, such as alcohol outlet density or area deprivation indices.

We could also imagine scraping the web for AA meeting times and locations.

As GPS sensing methods become more robust and missingness is reduced, GPS features may become more powerful for predicting lapse risk.

Future Directions

  • Expand risk features

  • Improve GPS sensing

  • Include individuals with non-abstinent recovery goals

Another thing I have been thinking about is how our models might generalize to individuals with non-abstinent recovery goals.

Our models were trained to predict goal-inconsistent use, which for all of our participants pursuing abstinence meant any alcohol use. The definition of goal-inconsistent use is less straightforward for individuals who do not aim to abstain and may vary considerably from person to person.

This may require considering alternative outcomes for our models to be trained on. Heavy drinking days are a common metric of risky or hazardous drinking. This may represent a more consistent marker of goal-inconsistent behavior across individuals in recovery.

Another option is to allow individuals to self-define what goal-inconsistent use is for them (e.g., drinking on a weekday, drinking more than planned) and then train models on samples of individuals with more diverse recovery goals.

Future Directions

  • Expand risk features

  • Improve GPS sensing

  • Include individuals with non-abstinent recovery goals

However, focusing exclusively on drinking or substance use as the primary outcome may overlook other clinically meaningful indicators of recovery. Outcomes such as well-being, mental health, functional status, and quality of life may be equally—if not more—important markers of progress. So we could even imagine changing our models from predicting lapse to predicting a marker of recovery health.

Lastly the area I am most excited about for immediate next steps is understanding how best to communicate our model outputs to individuals within the context of a recovery monitoring and support system.

For example, our model produces quantitative predictions of lapse probability. We could provide this as a continuous risk score.

Characterize these probabilities as trends. For example, whether risk this week is higher or lower than in the prior week.

Beyond risk probabilities, our models also identify the most influential features contributing to each prediction. We could provide feedback about which feature is putting them at highest risk for lapse.

We could also provide a recovery recommendation based on the most important feature.

In our lab, we are currently launching a study where we will look at what combination of risk-relevant information leads to optimal engagement and clinical recovery outcomes.

Its also exciting to think about how in that so distant future we may have the tools to feed our model outputs into a more fully autonomous support system capable of dynamically generating and delivering personalized recommendations, engaging individuals in interactive problem‑solving and conversation, and facilitating connections to therapists or peer support specialists when needed.
